🏥 Product + Data Engineering · TraySense · SYDE Capstone

Assisted surgical tray verification

📅 When: Sep 2024 – Apr 2025 👥 Team of 5 (my role: data + DB design) 🏢 University of Waterloo

In Canada, 21.6% of surgeries face delays due to Medical Device Reprocessing (MDR) errors. TraySense is a tray assembly verification system designed to reduce those errors. I scoped the problem, designed the data model, and ran the statistical analysis proving it works.

The problem space

What the MDR department actually deals with

The Medical Device Reprocessing department is the invisible backbone of surgical operations. Every surgical tray (containing dozens of specific instruments) must be decontaminated, reassembled correctly, and sterilised before it can be used. At Grand River Hospital, this cycle runs over 123,000 times a year.

📋 Static documentation

Depending on the hospital, tray assembly instructions are stored in paper booklets or Excel files. This leaves technicians to manually navigate 1900+ tray configurations.

🧠 Cognitive load

Technicians work under significant time pressure. 73% of Ontario medical technicians report burnout as pace, quantity, and quality are all critical.

💸 Real costs

$38k in tool overstock, ~$300k lost annually from surgery delays, and at least one costly error everyday. Each error can cascade; one delayed tray can delay multiple procedures.

Discovery

How we understood the problem

We didn't start with a solution. We started with observation. The team conducted an introductory call, a discovery tour, shadowing sessions, and formal user interviews with MDR leads and technicians at Grand River Hospital before writing a line of code.

We built a Hierarchical Task Analysis using verbal protocol with two experienced technicians across multiple tray reassemblies. This identified the exact workflow stages where things broke down:

⚠️ Bottleneck 1: Missing / incorrect items

When a tool was absent or wrong, the entire workflow halted. Technicians had to stop, search, and troubleshoot with no system to tell them what was missing or where it might be.

⚠️ Bottleneck 2: Sterile indicator validation

Verifying sterile indicators was a point of uncertainty during final checks. Technicians second-guessed themselves, adding time and stress to an already demanding workflow.

The solution

How TraySense works

TraySense tags surgical tools and trays, and scans them at assembly stations. Rather than full automation, this is a human-in-the-loop approach. The technology supports and validates the technician's work without replacing their judgement.

Real-time tool status for technicians

One design challenge: the scanner can only reliably read 10–12 tags simultaneously. We handled this with a stale/refresh rate: if a tag isn't detected for a specified number of scan cycles, it's marked "stale" and removed from the UI. This prevents phantom reads while accounting for tags that temporarily drop out of range.

Dta engineering

The MySQL schema

I designed the relational database schema that underpins the system. The core challenge was building something that could handle multiple levels of instrument identity simultaneously:

🔑 Type-level tracking

All scalpels share a ID for "scalpel" regardless of manufacturer, so you can query "does this tray have a scalpel" without caring which specific scalpel it is.

🔑 Item-level tracking

Each individual instrument also has its own ID, enabling individual history, defect tracking, and usage counts. This is what enables predictive maintenance.

The bigger picture: when a hospital switches vendors for a specific instrument, a single record update reflects across all records for that type of instrument. Without a relational DB, that change used to mean mean manually updating hundreds of Excel files.

User testing & statistics

What the data actually showed

I ran the full statistical analysis for our controlled user study: timed trials comparing manual assembly (A) vs TraySense (B) across users in two groups: those who started with A then switched to B (our modelled real-world scenario), and those who started with B then switched to A.

Why the A→B group is the one that matters

In real-world settings, technicians trained on manual assembly would transition to TraySense, exactly what the A-first group models. That group showed a 26.6s average improvement with a large effect size (0.89) and a p-value approaching significance (0.17) despite only 7 participants. The near-zero correlation (~0.01) means performance on the manual system doesn't predict TraySense performance, it's a different skill.
