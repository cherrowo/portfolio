What is the Aphrodite Project?

Aphrodite is a student-run dating platform built around a single constraint: one match per person per run. No swiping, no algorithm optimising for engagement, no infinite scroll. Participants fill out a 60-question questionnaire, and a stable-matching algorithm — based on work that won the 2012 Nobel Prize in Economics — pairs each person with one date.

The model is deliberately anti-addictive. It runs once, matches everyone it can, and closes. That scarcity is the product.

The Aphrodite homepage — a live sign-up counter ticks up during the open period, creating urgency without manufactured scarcity. The copy is direct: one match, 60 questions, 20 minutes.

chapter 1 · launch

Getting 3,500+ students to sign up in 12 hours

The Aphrodite launch is time-pressured by design. Sign-ups open, the window closes, and matches get released. Everything depends on getting enough students to sign up — ideally a diverse cross-section of the student population — within a tight window.

My job was coordinating the 2023 international launch: briefing regional launch managers, building the marketing calendar, writing copy across Instagram, Facebook, Discord, and Reddit, and personally managing the largest campus (Waterloo) which accounted for roughly 30% of total sign-ups.

The North American Instagram grid across the 2024 run — launch teaser, sign-up countdown ("One More Day!"), sign-up milestone ("Over 9,900 signed up!"), and match release announcement. Each post is timed to the campaign calendar.
The 2023 international campaign planning board — stories and posts localised across English, Traditional Chinese, and Cantonese for the Hong Kong and Singapore markets. Multi-region launches require coordinating tone, timing, and platform strategy across very different campus cultures.

A recurring challenge: making the messaging feel inclusive for LGBTQ+ students who assumed the platform was built for straight couples. Updating copy from "meet your ideal date" framing to "for students, by students" language — and being explicit about same-sex matching — increased LGBTQ+ sign-ups measurably.

chapter 2 · data science

Finding and measuring racial bias in the matching algorithm

The Aphrodite matching algorithm is based on stable matching — an algorithm designed to produce stable, preference-respecting pairings. But stable matching doesn't guarantee equitable outcomes when the underlying preference data reflects real-world biases. If enough participants set racial dealbreakers (explicitly or implicitly through preference choices), the algorithm faithfully executes those biases at scale.

My research question: is the algorithm producing racially disproportionate outcomes, and if so, how much of that is structural versus preference-driven?

Distribution by race across the participant pool. "This doesn't matter to me" was the most common selection by a significant margin — but the long tail of specific racial categories, and the variation in their frequencies, shaped the bias analysis. A small overrepresentation in one category can compound within the matching algorithm.

What "bias in stable matching" actually means

If Group A is underrepresented and also receives lower preference rankings on average, they accumulate to the end of the matching queue — and are disproportionately likely to go unmatched. Even small per-person preference skews produce large aggregate effects across thousands of matches. This is the phenomenon I was quantifying.

Exploration of average match scores (EPS — expected preference scores) to determine appropriate scaling values. The heavy right skew reveals that most participants receive very low scores in cross-group pairings — a key input for understanding how bias accumulates in the queue.

The analysis involved EDA across 20k+ user profiles, statistical testing to identify which demographic groups were systematically under-matched, and designing experiments to test whether preference score rescaling or queue reordering could reduce disparity without overriding stated preferences.

🔍 Exploratory analysis

Python, Pandas, matplotlib. Characterised the racial distribution of the participant pool, the distribution of racial dealbreakers, and the correlation between demographic features and match outcomes.

⚖️ Fairness experiments

Designed and ran experiments on preference score scaling to test whether normalising scores within demographic groups could reduce outcome disparity. Evaluated tradeoffs between fairness metrics and individual match quality.

📋 Reporting

Translated findings into actionable recommendations for the Aphrodite team and contributed to the annual data report shared with participants — making the research visible and accountable to the community it affected.

chapter 3 · safety UX

Designing safety features that don't make things worse

After a confidential incident involving a participant, the Aphrodite team made the decision to add formal safety infrastructure to the platform: a report feature, a resources page, and updated harassment guidelines. I was brought into the UX team to help design and write these features.

The core design challenge was language. Safety features on dating platforms often fall into one of two failure modes: either too clinical and alarming (making users feel surveilled), or too soft and euphemistic (making users feel the report won't be taken seriously). The goal was to be clear, direct, and human.

The safety onboarding modal — shown to all returning users before they can access their match. Introduces the three new safety features without alarming language. "Get started" rather than "I understand" was a deliberate choice to avoid a compliance-checkbox feel.
The harassment reporting flow — accessed through the match page's three-dot menu. Clear numbered steps, no jargon. "Tell us what is going on" rather than "describe the incident" — a small word choice that significantly changes the emotional register of the interaction.

🚨 Report feature

Direct reporting to the Aphrodite team from the match page. Accessible via the three-dot menu so it's available without being prominently visible — present when needed, not anxiety-inducing at first glance.

📚 Resources page

Region-aware links to support services. The key design decision: surfacing resources organised by region rather than by incident type, so users don't have to categorise their own experience to get help.

📋 Updated guidelines

Rewritten zero-tolerance harassment policy in plain language. Avoided legalese while making the consequences concrete. Copywriting objective: a student should know exactly what they can report and what happens next.

The hardest part of safety feature design isn't the interface — it's the words. Every phrase in a report modal carries weight for someone who is already in a difficult situation. I reviewed the copy multiple times with this question: "Does this make it easier or harder to ask for help?"

product analytics

Using Google Analytics to improve the product between runs

Between run cycles, Aphrodite's website still serves an important function — explaining the project to prospective participants, building trust, and converting curious students into sign-ups when the next run opens. I used Google Analytics to identify where people were leaving and what was working.

  • Engagement drop-off analysis — identified which pages had high exit rates and hypothesised whether the issue was content, navigation, or expectation mismatch
  • Off-season content strategy — proposed new content initiatives to keep the audience engaged between runs based on what existing content was retaining attention
  • Firebase data integrity — identified and resolved match data errors in Firebase that were causing incorrect match results to be surfaced to some participants
reflection

What four years on this project taught me

✦ Fairness is a design problem

Algorithmic bias isn't something that only machine learning engineers should care about. The Aphrodite research made it clear that design decisions — how preferences are framed, what options are offered, how the questionnaire structures choices — shape the bias that enters the system before the algorithm even runs.

✦ Tone is a product decision

The LGBTQ+ inclusion work and the safety feature copywriting both reinforced the same lesson: how you say something determines whether people feel the product is for them. Inclusive language isn't a nicety — it's a conversion driver and a trust signal.

✦ Data and design need each other

The bias research produced findings. Converting those findings into changes required understanding the UX of the questionnaire, the product logic of the matching system, and the psychology of how participants interpret preference questions. You can't fix a bias problem with data alone.

✦ Small platforms, real stakes

Aphrodite isn't a billion-user platform. But the stakes for the individual participants — a first date, a match that reflects or ignores their identity, a safety incident — are completely real. Working at small scale with real consequences is better training than working at massive scale where individual impact is invisible.
