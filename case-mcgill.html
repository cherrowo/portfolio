The research question

Why abandoned oil wells?

The US and Canada have over a million documented abandoned and orphaned oil wells. These are sites that already have infrastructure (roads, electrical connections, cleared land) and are already disturbed from a land-use perspective. That makes them potentially ideal candidates for renewable energy installation, if the energy potential is actually there.

The broader research project aimed to model total renewable energy capacity across wind, solar, and geothermal energies. My role was the data foundation: build a clean, validated well dataset, and then perform spatial analysis to assess energy potential site by site.

Data engineering

Cleaning 30M+ records across incompatible sources

The first problem was that no single source had a complete count of abandoned wells, especially ones that pre-date 1870. Government and industry sources have different reporting standards, different coverage years, different formatting. My job was to consolidate them into something research-usable.

🔧 The data pipeline

1

Source consolidation

Discovered and pulled records from multiple government and industry datasets. Wrote Python scripts to standardise formats (field names, date formats, coordinate systems, units) across all sources.

2

Cross-source validation

Compared counts between sources, flagging discrepancies above a 10% threshold. If two authoritative sources disagreed by more than 10% on a state/country's well count, the data needed further investigation before it could be trusted.

3

Deduplication

Removed 23k+ duplicate records using API numbers (the unique well identifier). The complication: sidetracks (directional branches off a primary well) share the same API10 digits. I removed duplicates based on API_original, then reconciled with the original paper's methodology. For Texas alone, the approach differed by ~6,000 wells, but with 300k+ Texas wells that's under 1% (acceptable).

4

Documentation

Converted all exploratory analyses into reproducible scripts with clear documentation. The dataset is used by other researchers on the team, and Python scripts reduced manual QA time by 90%.

Spatial analysis

Assessing energy potential site by site

With a clean dataset, I used QGIS to cross-reference each well location against public raster datasets for wind, solar, and geothermal potential. Each energy type had its own data challenges.

Eligible well sites by energy type

Number of abandoned/orphaned well sites meeting minimum generation thresholds (US estimates)

💨 Wind

2.88M sites meeting cut-in threshold (5.8 m/s at 100m)

Key challenge: the Global Wind Atlas reports power density at 10, 50, 100, 150, 200m, but not 80m, the standard hub height for many turbines. I interpolated between 50m and 100m values and used 80m wind speeds as the cutoff threshold.


Methodological challenge

Heights misaligned between power density data (missing 80m) and wind speed data. Required interpolation to bridge the gap.

☀️ Solar

1.80M threshold: >1600 kWh/kWp (high potential)

Irradiance data extracted from NETL. Used GHI (Global Horizontal Irradiance) for simplicity over DNI, which requires tracking panel position. Regulatory constraints (setbacks from roads, water, wildlife) are hard to assess systematically at scale.


Methodological challenge

No defined panel size standard (makes per-site output estimates impossible). Highly seasonal variation also complicates analysis.

🌋 Geothermal

120k abandoned + orphaned sites potentially eligible (US/CAN)

Geothermal calculations require temperature gradients at depth. Monthly surface temperature data available via NASA, but time-intensive to process at the scale of millions of sites.


Methodological challenge

Public geothermal data often only available as maps, not structured datasets (limits the precision of potential estimates).

Wind energy deep-dive

From a theoretical max to a realistic range

The wind analysis went through several iterations. From a rough theoretical maximum to a more grounded range based on specific turbine models.

Wind energy potential estimates (US abandoned well sites)

Three calculation approaches, from simplified to turbine-specific (all assume 1 turbine per eligible well site)

Approach 1: Simplified max

2,882,194 sites × 6 MW capacity (energy.gov estimate for 100m turbine) = 17 TW theoretical max. For reference, the world consumed 24,398 TWh of electricity in 2022. (ceiling, not a realistic number)

Approach 2: Efficiency-adjusted

Same site count, applying real-world loss factors: 5% wake losses, 30% efficiency, 0.2% mechanical, 1.5% electrical, 5% transmission, 3% downtime. Result: 2.88 TW (still large, significantly more realistic)

Approach 3: Specific turbine model

Used the Ecotecnia 100 turbine (100m hub height, speed-specific power ratings). Extrapolating from its power curve at average site wind speeds: 1.9 TW. (most conservative estimate)

Contribution to renewable goals

US goal: 100% carbon-free electricity by 2030.

Canada's goal: 90% renewable energy sources by 2030.

Even the conservative 1.9 TW estimate would be a meaningful contribution to both countries' targets if only a fraction were developed.

Analytical framework

Structuring the research for the team

Beyond the data work, I contributed an analytical framework document that proposed how the broader research team should handle several structural challenges:

  • Unit consistency: Wind (W/m²), solar (kWh/kWp), and geothermal (MWe/km²) use incompatible units. I proposed converting everything to MWh/year for direct comparison.
  • Regional vs. state-level analysis: 5 regions vs. 50 states + 12 provinces. I argued for state-level to account for variation while keeping the analysis manageable.
  • Seasonal correction: Wind and solar are highly seasonal. I proposed applying capacity factor adjustments by region and season rather than using annual averages.
  • Offshore wells: Flagged as requiring separate treatment. offshore infrastructure and regulation are fundamentally different from onshore.
