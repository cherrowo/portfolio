🎮 Information Architecture + Gamification · RehvUp

Designing a gamification system from scratch

📅 Sep – Dec 2023 👤 Information Architect / Designer 🏢 RehvUp

RehvUp is a workplace social platform (think Instagram for employee learning), where EX coaches post short-form video content on wellbeing and professional growth. I joined as the sole designer with two goals: fix what was broken, and increase engagement. The startup was acquired a few months after my work was implemented, which I think is a win on my behalf.

context

What is RehvUp?

RehvUp is an HR tech platform built around short-form video content for workplace wellbeing. The app has three distinct user types: employees, managers, and EX (Employee Experience) coaches who create video content on topics like mindfulness, feedback, and professional development within tailored communities.

👤
Employees

Primary content consumers. Watch videos, track progress, earn points.

👔
Managers

Assign content to teams, track completion, participate in communities.

🎥
EX Coaches

External experts who create short-form video content on the platform.

👥
Communities

Cross-functional groups with shared content feeds and discussion.

The platform had been live for some time but had accumulated significant UX debt. App logic was broken in ways that frustrated users. Navigation didn't match user mental models. And engagement was low. Users were opening the app but not returning.

The brief I was given was pretty vague: "improve engagement and fix user experience issues." No specific problem statement, no existing user research, no defined success metrics. A blank canvas is its own design challenge. Before you can solve anything, you have to decide what to solve.

diagnosis

Finding the real problems before designing the solutions

I started with structured app testing sessions. I went through the app systematically as each user type, documenting every moment of friction, confusion, broken logic, and unexpected behaviour. I also worked directly with the third-party engineering contractors who had built the app, which gave me access to the intended logic versus the actual behaviour which was a gap that explained most of the problems.

Navigation didn't match mental models

The information architecture had been built around the app's internal data structure, not how users thought about their tasks. Finding content required navigating through menus that made no sense unless you understood the backend.

Post engagement logic was broken

The logic controlling how content appeared in feeds and how engagement was tracked had errors that meant some content never surfaced and some interactions weren't being registered. Users weren't crazy, the app actually wasn't working.

No reason to return

Once a user had watched their assigned content, there was nothing pulling them back. No progress to track, no social layer, no sense of accumulation. The app had no longitudinal engagement hook.

gamification system design

Building the engagement loop

Gamification is easy to get wrong. Points and badges thoughtlessly slapped onto a product them feel hollow and condescending. I started by defining the system architecture: what the components were, how they connected, and what user behaviour each was meant to drive.

The target behaviours: post, comment, watch content, return on consecutive days, contribute to communities. The gamification system needed to make all of these feel rewarding without making the app feel like a video game. Workplace context matters. The tone had to be motivating without being juvenile.

⭐

Experience Points (XP)

Earned through app interactions: posting, commenting, watching content. Displayed on the user profile to visualise progress toward the next persona tier.

🧬

Personas (Rank Tiers)

Rank tiers unlocked by accumulated XP. Each persona has defined privileges and benefits. Progression should feel like it means something, not just a number going up.

🏆

Leaderboards

Weekly and monthly boards showing the most active users. Additional XP awarded to top performers, creating a healthy competitive loop without being punishing.

📊

Progress Dashboard

A profile-level view of points, badges earned, current rank, and progress to the next tier. Gives users a clear picture of where they are and what comes next.

🎯

Challenges

Short-term and long-term goals for bonus XP. Short challenges renew frequently to keep daily engagement up; longer ones reward sustained commitment.

🎖️

Milestone Badges

Goal-oriented badges tied to specific app milestones (first post, 30-day streak, etc). Gives users clear targets beyond XP.

📚

Educational Resources

FAQs, tutorials, and tips explaining how the gamification system works. Helps users understand how to maximise engagement, which is essential for any new system with no prior reference.

information architecture

Rebuilding the navigation around task completion

I ran analysis sessions with each user type, mapping their primary tasks and the number of taps required to complete them in the existing navigation versus a restructured one. The redesigned IA reduced task completion paths significantly across all user types and eliminated the most common navigation dead-ends that were generating support requests.

Before: content-type navigation

  • Videos (all videos, undifferentiated)
  • Communities (no task context)
  • Coaches (creator profiles, not content)
  • Profile (settings buried here)

After: task-oriented navigation

  • Home (my posted content, my progress)
  • Explore (browse by topic, featured content)
  • My Team / Community (social and leaderboard layer)
  • Profile (achievements, streaks, settings)
working with engineering

Fixing broken logic through design-engineering collaboration

The broken engagement logic wasn't something I could fix in Figma, it required direct collaboration with the third-party contractors who had built the app. This was an unusual part of the role: acting as an intermediary between the product vision, the broken implementation, and the engineering team who needed clear bug reports and behaviour specifications to fix it.

I documented every broken behaviour I found in structured bug reports: expected behaviour, actual behaviour, reproduction steps, and the user impact. This gave the engineering team actionable tickets rather than vague complaints, and it let me prioritise fixes by user impact rather than technical complexity.

New features don't fix broken foundations. The most impactful design work I did on this project was writing very clear bug reports.

delivery

Handing off a product roadmap, not just a design

At the end of the term I delivered more than Figma files. The full handoff included:

  • User personas — detailed profiles for each of the four user types, grounded in the app testing sessions and conversations with the team
  • Journey maps — current state and future state flows for each persona's primary use cases
  • Figma prototype — high-fidelity designs for the gamification system, the IA restructure, and the desktop companion interface
  • Product roadmap — prioritised features with success criteria, rollout sequencing, and a clear articulation of what's in scope for next phase versus later
  • Scope documentation — written rationale for every in/out-of-scope decision, so future designers and engineers understand the reasoning, not just the conclusion

The roadmap was used directly to guide the next two development sprints after my term ended. That's the goal: leave the product in a state where the next person knows what to do and why.

reflection

What this project taught me

✦ Diagnosis before prescription

The brief asked for gamification. The problem was broken logic. If I'd jumped straight to designing points and badges, I'd have shipped a delightful overlay on a broken experience. Taking time to diagnose before designing was the right call, giving me a solid foundation to build new features onto.

✦ Scope is important

Setting a clear scope boundary at the start of the project wasn't limiting, it let be intentional about what was in scope rather than shallow on everything.

✦ Gamification is a tricky beast

Points and streaks only work when the underlying product gives users something worth returning for. I designed the gamification system last because adding engagement hooks to a broken experience just accelerates frustration. Foundation first, incentives second.

✦ Engineering handoff is a design skill

Writing clear bug reports, scoping tickets accurately, and communicating design intent to contractors who weren't in the original design conversations is design work. The best Figma file in the world doesn't help if the engineers building from it don't understand the intent behind the choices.
