Rebuilding a 5,000-page website from first principles

📅 When: Sep – Dec 2021 👤 Role: Designer + Analyst 🏢 Where: TRIUMF: Canada's Particle Accelerator

TRIUMF's website had grown for decades without a coherent strategy. 5,000+ pages, two competing nav bars, and a fragmented relationship between the main site and facility microsites. I conducted the full research and produced a strategic roadmap for fixing it.

The situation

What TRIUMF's website looked like

TRIUMF is Canada's national particle accelerator centre, a sprawling research institution with multiple facilities, dozens of divisions, and audiences ranging from academic collaborators to the general public to incoming talent. The website had to serve all of them, and was failing most of them.

🌐 External users: disengaged

  • Most news articles dated from 2020. Not "we're a cutting-edge research centre" energy
  • Content presented as a text stream with small visuals which is not competitive with modern science communication
  • No clear hierarchy between TRIUMF's multiple external sites (main site, TRIUMF50, DOL, 5YP)
  • Unclear what the site was supposed to do: attract commercial partners? talent? public engagement? file storage? Everyone was thrown under one site

🔬 Internal users: lost

  • High-frequency internal users made up half the traffic but they were only ever visiting 5–6 pages (directory, quick-links, admin, workday)
  • Facility-specific microsites linked back to triumf.ca for certain tools, so the same information might be on the facility page, the main site, Docushare, or Office365
  • Two competing nav bars made traversal confusing. The sidebar changed as you moved through the site
Data analysis

What Google Analytics revealed

I analysed site traffic data collected by Google Analytics over October 2021, chosen deliberately. October is a low-volatility month: Thanksgiving is the only major holiday, fewer co-op students cycling in and out, and using past data meant numbers would stay accurate throughout analysis. The findings were organised into three testable hypotheses.

I structured the analysis around hypotheses rather than just describing the data. The hypotheses forced the data to answer specific questions about user intent and structural failure.

🔍 Hypothesis 1: TRIUMF is getting external traffic! but failing to keep it.

The homepage wasn't offering anything of value to a majority of external visitors.

  • A staggering proportion of total users were new users (high external reach with no retention)
  • The landing page absorbed a majority of all site traffic, but out of 5,000+ pages, most got under 100 views/month
  • Bounce rates were 41.7% for the landing page and 59.1% for the homepage (Google Analytics tracks them separately but they're functionally the same page). Nearly half of visitors left immediately
  • The site-wide average bounce rate was 46%
  • Pages with the highest engagement times were internal directory pages. Externally-facing content underperformed consistently
  • Sankey flow diagrams showed new users overwhelmingly leaving after one page (high bounce rate, few pages visited, low engagement time)

Bounce rate by page type

External-facing pages bounce far more than internal utility pages, confirming the site isn't serving public visitors

🔍 Hypothesis 2: Internal users only use a handful of pages

They have their own tools for everything else and actively avoid triumf.ca's confusing navigation.

  • Quick-links, Directory, and Workday dominated internal traffic (high unique pageviews, low bounce rates, high exit rates). People get the link they need and leave
  • Sankey diagrams showed returning users accessing one resource and leaving with many bypassing the landing page entirely, going directly to Workday, Quick-links, Directory, or Training pages from saved bookmarks
  • User Explorer revealed a significant cohort of 200+ session users in a single month. These are people for whom triumf.ca is essentially a utility app, not a website
  • At least 6 facility-specific microsites (cycops, cmms, titan, ccn, mis, bnmr) existed outside the GA data, meaning some important internal links didn't require navigating triumf.ca at all

User behaviour concentration for triumf.ca

High-frequency internal users cluster almost entirely on utility pages. External users spread across (and quickly leave) the rest, usually the landing page

🔍 Hypothesis 3: Most visitors already intended to come to TRIUMF.ca

Traffic channels heavily implied existing affiliation with TRIUMF this wasn't random search traffic stumbling in.

  • "Direct" was the dominant channel (people typing "triumf.ca" directly), meaning intentional visits
  • "Organic Search" meant people specifically searching for TRIUMF
  • Many referral sites were internal, traffic was coming from other TRIUMF sites
  • Demographics supported this: a majority of users were Canada-based, Canadian users had the lowest bounce rate, and the largest identified city cluster was the Vancouver area (where TRIUMF is located)
  • Top interest categories matched TRIUMF workforce demographics: session time increased with age (older = established science career = work-related use)
  • Language breakdown: three forms of English dominated; Japanese was a notable secondary (consistent with TRIUMF's known Japanese researcher partnerships)
  • Peak usage times corresponded directly to working hours (midday spikes, low early-morning and late-night activity. TRIUMF has excellent work-life balance)

A methodological catch: The internal traffic filter in GA wasn't working (the numbers implied zero internal users), which directly contradicted everything the behavioural data showed. This is a data quality issue: the filter was broken, meaning the actual split between internal and external traffic was unknown and all numbers needed to be read with that in mind.

Competitive analysis

Three organisations that also handle splits across content offerings

Not all competitive analysis means direct competitors. It means finding organisations solving analogous (similar structure and function, that's my word of the day) problems. I chose each of these for a specific reason.

MIT Media Lab
Best candidate for content structure + newsroom design
Experimental research facility Non-profit Decentralised divisions General + specialist audiences

Key takeaways: Visual-first newsroom with large imagery and large text (contrast to TRIUMF's text-stream format). Clean internal/external split with a well-defined portal. Intuitive research topic navigation that doesn't require knowing division names upfront.

Adobe
Best candidate for complex product/tool navigation patterns
Large product catalogue Multiple audience types Extensive use of footer as secondary nav

Key takeaways: Organises products by usage type (Creativity & Design, PDF & e-Signatures, Marketing) rather than product name. Directly applicable to TRIUMF's research programmes. Extensive footer architecture surfaces everything without cluttering the primary nav. Showcases top products on homepage with a repeat in the footer.

Bruce Power
Best candidate for closest direct comparison
Also produces isotopes Same dual audience: talent + commercial partners Science + public safety messaging

Key takeaways: Homepage simultaneously addresses commercial partners and incoming talent while also including social cause messaging (decarbonization, anti-racism) that resonates with a younger academic audience. Clear career and commercial pathways in the nav. Highlights specific major programmes (NetZero2025, Life-Extension) to signal credibility and ambition.

Core recommendation

The internal / external split

The structural fix is a clean separation: a lean, purpose-built external site for public audiences, and a proper internal portal for staff. This is how most large organisations handle this problem, and it's what TRIUMF's analytics data was pointing to anyway.

I mapped out three possible approaches for handling the facility microsites:

Option A: Consolidate within facility pages

All internal content lives on facility-specific microsites; triumf.ca stays lean for external audiences.

✓ Keeps triumf.ca simple · ✓ Facilities maintain ownership

✗ Requires updating decades-old pages · ✗ Creates inconsistent experience across facilities

Option B: Centralise in a single internal portal Recommended

Migrate all internal tools and content to a unified portal. Decommission facility microsites over time.

✓ Single source for resources · ✓ Can decommission old sites · ✓ Best long-term scalability

✗ Significant migration effort · ✗ Requires buy-in from all facility stakeholders

Option C: Status quo, with pruning

Keep the current relationship but remove unneeded content from triumf.ca with consideration for facility needs.

✓ Lowest disruption · ✓ Fastest to implement

✗ Doesn't solve the root problem · ✗ Technical debt accumulates further

External content strategy

The CURE framework

For the external-facing side, I proposed a four-step repeatable content strategy rather than a one-time redesign. The goal was to give the team a process they could apply to any section of the site, not just a single output.

C - Curate

Select and prioritise content for a public audience (not every internal announcement belongs on the external site). Define a content brief for each section: who is this for, what do we want them to do?

U - Update

Make content current and relevant. Most news articles on the site were outdated. For a cutting-edge research facility, that's a credibility problem. Set a content freshness standard.

R - Redesign

Move from text streams to visual-first layouts modelled on MIT's newsroom: large images correlated with stories, bigger typography, visual hierarchy that works at a glance rather than requiring reading.

E - Evaluate

Use A/B testing and analytics to measure whether changes increased engagement. Build the feedback loop from the start. Don't treat the redesign as a one-off event.

Change management

Communicating a major redesign

A recommendation that ignores implementation tends not to get implemented. A significant section of my proposal covered how to communicate the changes, because the Qualtrics survey data showed staff already felt communication gaps existed.

  • Involve facility stakeholders in the design process early! Not just as sign-off recipients but as active participants in shaping their piece of the new website
  • Distribute progress updates through existing channels: sitewide emails, division leaders passing information to teams, Microsoft Teams
  • Prepare FAQs and tutorials before the launch, not after questions arrive. Large changes always generate large question volumes
  • Be transparent: if people have concerns about the redesign, welcome them. Forced changes on an internal audience rarely stick

The internal comms satisfaction data made this concrete. The lowest-scoring dimension was amount of information, not clarity or tone. People wanted more updates, more often.
